ABSTRACT

The capitalist system is the driving force of the commodification of urban spaces and culture, and in turn has transformed a plethora of communal areas into profit-hungry commodities, seeing the erasure of their function and original purpose as locations for resistance, belonging and creativity.

This thesis, aims to investigate how capitalism prioritises the commercial interests, within urban development, over what is good for the neighbourhood - or public good. This dynamic frequently results in gentrification, the privatisation of public spaces, and a loss of authenticity in cultural expressions. To deepen this understanding, the thesis examines the concept of community by exploring key ideas through the lens of existing theories. The focus is on demonstrating how the capitalist system has subverted and twisted the word, ‘community’ to become another avenue for profit. Case studies of urban spaces are used to illustrate these dynamics and their real-world consequences.

By addressing the global intersections of urban space, culture, community and capitalism, this thesis is dedicated to representing a desire to reclaim spaces that are dedicated to the fostering of creativity, connection and expression.
